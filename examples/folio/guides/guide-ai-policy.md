# Guide: Understanding Your AI Policy

> **What this is:** A plain-English explanation of your `AI_POLICY.md` file — what each section means and why it matters.
>
> **Who it's for:** Anyone in the business who uses AI tools. Share this alongside the policy itself.

---

## Why you have an AI policy

AI tools are powerful — and that's exactly why a policy matters. Without clear rules, people use AI differently in ways that can expose the business to:

- **Confidentiality risk** — pasting client data, contracts, or internal financials into a public AI tool
- **Quality risk** — publishing AI output that's factually wrong, off-brand, or legally problematic
- **Consistency risk** — some people using AI heavily, others not at all, producing uneven work quality

A policy doesn't restrict AI use — it makes AI use safe and effective by setting shared expectations.

## Section by section

### Approved tools

This lists which AI tools are officially approved for use at work.

**Why it matters:** Different AI tools have different data handling policies. Some store your conversations to train future models. Some offer enterprise versions with stronger privacy guarantees. Only approved tools have been assessed for compliance with your data obligations.

If a tool isn't on the list, ask before using it for anything work-related.

---

### What you can use AI for

This lists the approved use cases — things like drafting content, summarising documents, generating first drafts of code, and running analysis.

**Why it matters:** This isn't meant to be exhaustive. It's giving you a clear starting point so you don't have to second-guess whether AI is appropriate for a task. When in doubt, check the policy or ask.

---

### What requires review before publishing

Anything that goes out to a client, gets posted publicly, or affects a business decision must be reviewed by a human before use.

**Why it matters:** AI makes mistakes. It can confidently state things that are factually wrong. It can write text that looks polished but misrepresents your product or makes claims you can't back up. Review is the safety net.

"I used AI to draft it" is not a reason to skip review — it's a reason to review it carefully.

---

### What's off limits

This is the list of things you should never do with AI tools.

The most important ones:

**Never paste client data into a public AI tool.** This typically means any personally identifiable information (names, emails, addresses), financial data, contracts, or any information shared with you in confidence. If you're not sure whether something counts as client data, treat it as if it does.

**Never paste internal business information that would be damaging if public.** Unannounced pricing, internal financial figures, staff issues, legal matters — these stay out of AI tools.

**Never rely on AI for legal or compliance decisions.** AI can help you think through problems, but it is not a lawyer. Anything with legal weight needs to go through the appropriate person.

---

### Confidentiality and data handling

Different AI tools handle your data differently:

- **Public/free tools** (consumer ChatGPT, Claude.ai free tier): your inputs may be used to improve the model unless you opt out. Check the tool's privacy settings.
- **Enterprise/paid tools** (ChatGPT Enterprise, Claude for Teams): data handling is typically contractually protected.
- **Local models** (Ollama, LM Studio): data never leaves your machine — highest privacy.

The policy specifies which category each approved tool falls into and what that means for what you can put into it.

---

### Who's responsible for what

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| **AI policy owner** | Keeps the policy updated; fields questions; reviews incidents |
| **Team leads** | Ensure their team knows and follows the policy |
| **Every user** | Responsible for their own AI use; must flag incidents |

If AI-generated content causes a problem — factual error, client complaint, data incident — the person who published it without adequate review is accountable, not the AI tool.

---

### Keeping the policy current

AI tools and capabilities change quickly. The policy has a review cadence — typically every 6–12 months or when a significant new tool is adopted. The AI policy owner is listed in your `AI_POLICY.md` with their contact.

If something in the policy feels unclear, outdated, or you've encountered a situation it doesn't cover, tell the policy owner. The policy improves through use.

## Quick reference

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|-------|---------|
| Use approved tools for approved tasks | Paste client personal data into any AI tool |
| Review all AI output before publishing | Publish AI-generated content without checking it |
| Use AI to speed up your first draft | Trust AI facts without verifying them |
| Keep your Master Prompt updated | Use unapproved tools for work tasks |
| Ask if you're unsure | Ignore incidents — report them |
