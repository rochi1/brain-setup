# Brand Voice — Folio

---

## Voice Summary

Folio sounds like a smart, experienced colleague who used to work at an agency and got fed up with the same problems you're dealing with. We're direct without being blunt, confident without being arrogant, and warm without being sycophantic. We know this world well enough to skip the pleasantries and get to the point. We take the work seriously. We don't take ourselves too seriously.

---

## Tone Descriptors

**We sound:**
- Direct
- Knowing (like we've seen this before)
- Warm
- Dry (occasionally)
- Honest

**We never sound:**
- Corporate
- Salesy or hype-y
- Vague or hedging
- Overly formal
- Like we're trying too hard to be clever

**Notes on formality:** We use contractions freely. We address people as "you". We write the way a smart person talks, not the way they write formal documents. We're never stuffy. We can be a bit dry when the situation calls for it, but we're not trying to do stand-up comedy either.

## Language Rules

### Words and phrases we use

| Use this | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| "deliver work" / "delivering work" | Not "ship deliverables" or "output content" |
| "your clients" | Not "end users" or "stakeholders" |
| "approval" | Not "sign-off process" — the word is cleaner |
| "portal" | Our product vocabulary — not "workspace" or "hub" |
| "agency" | Not "firm" or "studio" in most contexts |
| "feels like" | We talk about experience, not just functionality |
| Plain numbers | "save 3 hours a week" not "achieve significant time savings" |

### Words and phrases we avoid

| Avoid this | Use instead |
|------------|-------------|
| "leverage" | "use" |
| "streamline" | describe specifically what gets faster or easier |
| "seamless" | describe the actual experience |
| "best-in-class" | show, don't assert |
| "solution" | "tool", "product", or just name what it does |
| "empower" | too vague, too corporate — say what people can actually do |
| "game-changer" | never |
| "we're excited to announce" | just announce it |
| "at the end of the day" | cut it |
| "synergy" | not in any context, ever |

### Industry jargon
We use agency-world terminology freely because we're talking to agency people: deliverables, rounds of feedback, client sign-off, creative brief, revision, scope creep, retainer. We don't use SaaS startup jargon or enterprise software vocabulary.

## Grammar & Formatting Preferences

| Rule | Our preference |
|------|---------------|
| Oxford comma | Yes |
| Headline capitalisation | Sentence case |
| Exclamation marks | Rarely — only when genuinely deserved |
| Emoji | Occasionally in social and informal comms, never in product copy or serious content |
| Numbers | Numerals for everything (3 not three, 10 not ten) |
| Sentence length | Shorter when making a point. Varied overall. |
| Paragraph length | 1–3 sentences. White space is good. |
| Active vs. passive voice | Always active |
| Person | We/you — first and second person |

---

## DO / DON'T Table

| ✅ DO | ❌ DON'T |
|-------|---------|
| Lead with the problem we're solving, not the feature | Open with "We're excited to introduce..." |
| Use specific numbers — "saves 3 hours a week" | Use vague claims — "saves significant time" |
| Name the pain directly — "email threads that go nowhere" | Soften it into corporate-speak |
| Write short sentences when making a key point | Bury the point in a long paragraph |
| Let dry humour in when it fits naturally | Force jokes or be try-hard about being funny |
| Be honest when something is limited or hard | Hype everything into perfection |
| Address the reader's scepticism head-on | Pretend objections don't exist |
| Write "you'll" and "we've" — contractions are fine | Write "you will" and "we have" unless emphasis needs it |
| End emails and posts with a clear next step | End with "please don't hesitate to reach out" |
| Describe what the client *experiences*, not just what the feature *does* | List feature specs without translating to benefit |
| Acknowledge when something is a real problem for our customers | Dismiss pain points we haven't solved yet |
| Trust the reader to be smart | Over-explain or use a condescending tone |

## Voice in Practice — Before / After Examples

### Example 1: Product announcement

**Generic AI voice:**
"We are thrilled to announce the launch of our new inline video feedback feature, which will revolutionise the way creative agencies collect and manage client feedback on video deliverables."

**Folio voice:**
"You can now leave feedback directly on video. Timestamp it, draw on the frame, reply to your team — all without exporting anything or firing up a separate tool. If you've ever tried to explain a revision over email, you'll know why this matters."

---

### Example 2: Marketing headline

**Generic AI voice:**
"Streamline your client communication and project delivery with our all-in-one platform for creative professionals."

**Folio voice:**
"Stop losing hours to email threads your clients forget to reply to."

---

### Example 3: Handling a limitation honestly

**Generic AI voice:**
"While we currently don't offer enterprise SSO, we're always working to improve our feature set based on customer feedback and look forward to sharing more updates soon."

**Folio voice:**
"We don't have SSO yet — it's on the roadmap, but we won't commit to a date we're not confident in. If SSO is a hard requirement, we're probably not the right fit for you right now and we'd rather you know that upfront."

## Channel-Specific Adjustments

| Channel | Adjustments from default voice |
|---------|-------------------------------|
| Website copy | Tightest and most considered. Shorter sentences. Every word earns its place. |
| Product UI & microcopy | Even more minimal. Functional first. One job per piece of copy. |
| Marketing emails | Slightly more conversational. Can have a little more personality. |
| Transactional emails | Functional. No personality needed. Just clear. |
| Social media (LinkedIn) | More considered than casual. Can be a bit more opinionated and direct. |
| Support responses | Warmer. More patient. Solution-first — acknowledge before explaining. |
| Blog / long-form | Can breathe more. Room for examples, stories, a bit more texture. |

## Reference Material
