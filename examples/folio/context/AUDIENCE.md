## Persona 1 — The Overwhelmed Agency Owner

> "Sam" — Owner of a 12-person branding studio who started as a designer and never signed up for this much admin.

### Who they are
Founder and owner of an independent creative agency, typically 8–20 staff. Usually a designer, strategist, or producer by background who built a successful practice and now spends more time on account management and operations than on the work they love. Has been running the agency for 5–15 years. Revenue in the $800K–$3M range.

### Their situation
Sam has 6–12 active clients at any given time. Each one has its own folder structure, feedback rhythm, and preferred way of communicating. Some email, some use WhatsApp, one insists on a weekly call just to relay information that could have been a message. Sam's team is good but frustrated — they spend too long chasing approvals and tracking down feedback that should have taken 10 minutes but stretched to 3 days. Sam knows this is a business problem (delayed approvals delay invoices) but hasn't had the time or headspace to systematically fix it.

### Their primary goal
Run a profitable agency that doesn't depend entirely on Sam being in the room. Create systems that let the team deliver great work without Sam being the connective tissue for every client relationship.

### Their pain points
- Client feedback arrives in 4 different places and no one format
- Approvals drag — and late approvals mean late invoices
- Feels embarrassed sending clients to a shared Dropbox folder when competitors look more polished
- Has tried Notion and Basecamp — both too internal-facing, clients found them confusing
- Can't easily show a client the history of a project when there's a dispute
- Senior designers are spending 20% of their time on client coordination, not design

### What they need from us
A way to package and present their work that makes them look as professional as the output they actually produce. A clear, simple space for clients to review and approve — that doesn't require the client to create an account, learn a new tool, or reply to an email.

### How they talk
- "We need to get out of each other's inboxes"
- "I'm spending half my week on stuff that has nothing to do with design"
- "By the time the client responds, we've lost the momentum"
- "Our work is really good. Our process looks like a mess."
- "I just want the client to be able to see everything in one place"

### What they're skeptical about
- Another tool the clients won't actually use ("our clients are not tech-savvy")
- Complexity — they've been burned by tools that required a week of setup
- Integration with how they already work — don't want to rebuild their whole process
- Price relative to the time they already spend not fixing the problem

### Where we reach them
Agency owner communities (Bureau of Digital, Clients & Coffee, Design Twitter/X), LinkedIn, agency podcasts, referrals from other agency owners, Google ("client portal for agencies").

### How they make decisions
Usually fast — Sam is the decision-maker and doesn't need committee approval. Trials on their own, often converts within 7 days or not at all. Price sensitivity is moderate — they care more about whether it actually solves the problem than about $50/month. Word of mouth carries enormous weight.

## Persona 2 — The Agency Project Manager

> "Jordan" — Senior project manager at a 25-person digital agency, responsible for keeping 8 active projects moving.

### Who they are
Experienced project manager or account manager at an established agency, 3–8 years in the role. Reports to the agency owner or operations director. Manages the day-to-day of client relationships and project delivery — they're the person whose inbox is always at 200 unread.

### Their situation
Jordan lives in their task management tool (Linear, Asana, or ClickUp) for internal work, but the client-facing layer is still a mess of email threads and shared drives. They're the person who has to send "just following up on the above" emails 3 times before getting a decision. They know more than anyone what the approval bottlenecks are costing the agency in real money and real frustration.

### Their primary goal
Keep projects on time and clients informed without having to manually chase everything. Get faster, cleaner feedback so they can keep the team moving.

### Their pain points
- Chasing approvals is the job they hate most — feels like babysitting
- Feedback across different people in a client org arrives piecemeal and contradicts itself
- No single source of truth for what's been approved and when
- Weekly status emails take 30 minutes each to write and clients often don't read them

### What they need from us
A place to send clients where everything they need is waiting for them — and where Jordan gets notified the moment something is reviewed or approved instead of waiting to find out by checking their inbox.

### How they talk
- "I just need them to tell me yes or no"
- "I'm the one who has to explain to the designer why we're on round four"
- "Clients say they're reviewing it but I have no idea if they've even opened it"
- "If I didn't follow up, this project would just stop"

### What they're skeptical about
Whether the client will actually use it (the same concern Sam has, but more acute because Jordan is the one dealing with it when they don't).

### Where we reach them
Usually not the one who discovers Folio — typically introduced to it by the agency owner (Sam) who bought it. Becomes an advocate quickly if it actually reduces their chasing workload.

## Who We Are Not For

- **Enterprises looking for SSO, custom SLAs, or legal review** — we're SMB-focused and honest about it
- **Agencies whose clients are also technical teams** — those clients tend to have opinions about tooling and often want to use their own systems
- **Solo freelancers doing very occasional project work** — the overhead of a portal isn't worth it for one project a quarter
- **Agencies who want a single tool to replace everything** — we're a client-facing layer, not an all-in-one platform. If someone wants one tool for everything, we'll lose to ClickUp.

## Customer Lifecycle Stages

| Stage | What they know about us | What they need | How we communicate |
|-------|------------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Unaware | Nothing | To recognise the problem we solve | Problem-aware content — "why email feedback kills creative projects" |
| Aware | We exist as a client portal tool | To understand if we're meaningfully different from Notion/Basecamp | Direct comparison content, specific feature proof |
| Considering | What we offer and roughly how we're priced | To see it working in a real agency context | Case studies, interactive demo, free trial |
| Customer | They've bought and onboarded | To get value in the first 2 weeks before the novelty wears off | Onboarding sequence, check-in from CS at day 7 |
| Loyal | Deep experience | To grow their use (more clients, more seats) or refer us | Expansion conversations, referral programme |
